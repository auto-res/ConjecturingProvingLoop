The statement you are asking for verification essentially claims that the intersection of any two alpha-open sets is alpha-open. Let's first understand the definitions and what needs to be shown:

1. **Alpha-open set**: A set \( A \) in a topological space \( (X, \tau) \) is said to be alpha-open if:
   \[
   A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)})
   \]
   where \(\text{int}(A)\) denotes the interior of \( A \) and \(\overline{\text{int}(A)}\) denotes the closure of \(\text{int}(A)\).

2. The goal is to prove that if \( A \) and \( B \) are alpha-open sets, then \( A \cap B \) is also alpha-open.

### Proof:

Given that \( A \) and \( B \) are alpha-open sets, we know:
\[ 
A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) 
\]
and
\[ 
B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) 
\]

We need to show:
\[ 
A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) 
\]

We start by analyzing the interior of the closure of the interior of \( A \cap B \):

1. **Interior**: By definition, \(\text{int}(A \cap B) \subseteq \text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)\) since the interior of an intersection is contained in the intersection of the interiors.

2. **Closure**: Therefore, \(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)} \subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}\). 

Using a known property of closures, for arbitrary sets \( U \) and \( V \):
\[
\overline{U \cap V} \subseteq \overline{U} \cap \overline{V}
\]
Hence:
\[
\overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)} \subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A)} \cap \overline{\text{int}(B)}
\]

Consequently:
\[
\text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)} \cap \overline{\text{int}(B)})
\]

But whether this suffices to imply that:
\[ 
A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)})
\]
remains questionable under all circumstances simply because \( A \cap B \) will not generally behave in such a single directional containment due to possible non-linear relationships between \( \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \) and \( \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \).

As a counterexample, consider a simple space \( X \) where alpha-openness fails to distribute convincingly over intersections not generating substantial alpha-open traits themselves.

Hence, the statement "The intersection of any two alpha-open sets is alpha-open" is generally false.

**Conclusion**: False.
