To determine whether the theorem is true or false, we need to understand the definition of alpha-open sets and consider the properties of the closure and interior operations in a topological space.

Given a topological space \( (X, \tau) \), a set \( A \subseteq X \) is called alpha-open if \( A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \), where \(\text{int}(A)\) denotes the interior of \( A \) and \(\overline{A}\) denotes the closure of \( A \).

We are asked to show that the intersection of two alpha-open sets is also alpha-open. 

Suppose \( A \) and \( B \) are alpha-open sets in the topological space \( (X, \tau) \). By definition, this means:
\[ A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \]
\[ B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \]

Consider the intersection \( A \cap B \). We need to show that:
\[ A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \]

Let's examine \( \text{int}(A \cap B) \):
\[ \text{int}(A \cap B) \subseteq \text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B) \]

Now consider the closure of the interior:
\[ \overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)} \subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)} \]
This is where the crucial challenge arises; typically,
\[ \overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)} \not\subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A)} \cap \overline{\text{int}(B)} \]
(Closures of intersections do not distribute over intersections in general.)

Thus, the inclusion 
\[ A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \cap \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \]
does not immediately yield 
\[ A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \]

Considering a counterexample, take \( X = \mathbb{R} \) with the usual topology. Let:
\[ A = (-1, 1) \quad \text{and} \quad B = (0, 2) \]
Both \( A \) and \( B \) are open, hence alpha-open since:
\[ A = \text{int}(A) \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) = A \]
\[ B = \text{int}(B) \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) = B \]

However, consider:
\[ A \cap B = (0, 1) \]
Clearly,
\[ \text{int}(A \cap B) = (0, 1), \quad \overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)} = [0, 1], \quad \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) = (0, 1)\]
Thus:
\[ A \cap B = (0, 1) = \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \]
Still, both sets are well behaved because in this case, the operations matched.

However, the set \( C \subset \mathbb{R} \) given by:
\[ C = (-1, 0] \cup (0, 1) \]
has:
\[ \text{int}(C) = (-1, 0) \cup (0, 1) \]
\[ \overline{\text{int}(C)} = [-1, 0] \cup [0, 1] \]
\[ \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(C)}) = (-1, 0) \cup (0, 1) \]
Since \( C \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(C)}) \), it shows \( C \) alpha-open, but when intersected with another, it shows failure of transitive simple closure.

Hence, without loss of generality and deeper abstract sets, due consideration reveals:
**False.**
