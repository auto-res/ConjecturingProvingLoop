To prove this theorem, we first need to understand what it means for a set to be alpha-open. Given a topological space \( X \) and a subset \( A \subseteq X \), the set \( A \) is said to be alpha-open if:

\[
A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)})
\]

where \( \text{int}(A) \) denotes the interior of \( A \) and \( \overline{B} \) denotes the closure of the set \( B \).

Now, we want to show that if \( A \) and \( B \) are alpha-open sets in \( X \), then their intersection \( A \cap B \) is also an alpha-open set.

1. Assume \( A \) and \( B \) are alpha-open sets. Then:

\[
A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \quad \text{and} \quad B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)})
\]

We are required to show that:

\[
A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)})
\]

2. First, consider the interior of the intersection:

\[
\text{int}(A \cap B) \subseteq \text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)
\]

Using the closure properties:

\[
\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)} \subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}
\]

3. Furthermore, note that:

\[
\text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}) \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \cap \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)})
\]

Since \( A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \) and \( B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \), it follows:

\[
A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \cap \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)})
\]

Because \( A \cap B \) is a subset of both, this implies:

\[
A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}\left(\overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}\right)
\]

Hence also:

\[
A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)})
\]

Thus, the intersection \( A \cap B \) is alpha-open.

Hence, the theorem is true. The intersection of any two alpha-open sets is indeed alpha-open.
