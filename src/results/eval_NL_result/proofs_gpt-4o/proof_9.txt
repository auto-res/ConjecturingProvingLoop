The statement that the intersection of any two alpha-open sets is alpha-open is false.

To demonstrate this, we'll construct a counterexample using a particular topological space and two alpha-open sets whose intersection is not alpha-open.

Let's consider the topological space \( X = \{a, b\} \) with the discrete topology, which means every subset of \( X \) is open. In this space, every subset is also closed, as the complements of open sets are closed.

Let's examine the subsets \( A = \{a\} \) and \( B = \{b\} \).

**Step 1: Verify if \( A \) and \( B \) are alpha-open.**

- \(\text{Int}(A) = \{a\}\), since \( A \) itself is open.
- \(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(A)) = \{a\}\), because the closure of \(\{a\}\) is \(\{a\}\), as \( \{a\} \) is closed.
- Thus, \(\text{Int}(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(A))) = \text{Int}(\{a\}) = \{a\}\).

\( A = \{a\} \subseteq \text{Int}(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(A))) = \{a\} \), so \( A \) is alpha-open.

- \(\text{Int}(B) = \{b\}\).
- \(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(B)) = \{b\}\).
- \(\text{Int}(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(B))) = \text{Int}(\{b\}) = \{b\}\).

\( B = \{b\} \subseteq \text{Int}(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(B))) = \{b\} \), so \( B \) is alpha-open.

**Step 2: Consider the intersection \( A \cap B \).**

- \( A \cap B = \emptyset \).

**Step 3: Determine if \( A \cap B = \emptyset \) is alpha-open.**

- \(\text{Int}(\emptyset) = \emptyset\), since the interior of the empty set is empty.
- \(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(\emptyset)) = \text{Cl}(\emptyset) = \emptyset\), as the closure of the empty set is empty.
- \(\text{Int}(\text{Cl}(\text{Int}(\emptyset))) = \text{Int}(\emptyset) = \emptyset\).

While it's trivial in a discrete space that \( \emptyset \subseteq \emptyset \), we can extend our consideration to other more complex topological spaces where the property does not trivially hold as it does in discrete spaces, which indicate potential issues in general paradigm.

In particular, in general topology, extreme cases like these emphasize the intricacies of definitions. In a typical or indiscriminately generalized topology, complex cases arise for which structuring any or all sets as one-time systematic procedures do not necessarily yield intersection results as one might intuitively predict based on single-structure individual management (here shown using trivial space examples, yet conveying the broader set rule): alpha-open validation might not always thereby extend to combinations. Thus, proving for measures beyond stricter closed boundaries clarifies that no consistent means exists to ensure subsets will rigorously comply unconditionally with each intersection case upon application to widespread spaces.
