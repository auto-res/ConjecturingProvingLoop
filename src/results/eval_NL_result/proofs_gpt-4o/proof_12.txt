To determine whether the intersection of any two alpha-open sets is alpha-open in a topological space, let's first clarify what an alpha-open set is.

Given a topological space \( X \) and a subset \( A \subseteq X \), denote the interior of \( A \) by \( \text{int}(A) \) and the closure by \( \overline{A} \). A set \( A \) is defined as alpha-open if:

\[ A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}). \]

We need to prove that the intersection of two alpha-open sets is alpha-open. Let \( A \) and \( B \) be two alpha-open sets in \( X \).

By definition, we have:

\[ A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \]
and
\[ B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}). \]

Consider the intersection \( A \cap B \).

For \( A \cap B \) to be alpha-open, we need:

\[ A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}). \]

Let's analyze \( \text{int}(A \cap B) \). Since interior is not distributive over intersection, \( \text{int}(A \cap B) \subseteq \text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B) \). 

Now look at the closure:

\[ \overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)} \subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}. \]

However, the closure of the intersection is not generally equal to the intersection of the closures, so:

\[ \overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)} \not\subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A)} \cap \overline{\text{int}(B)}. \]

The interior of a closure can also have discrepancies:

\[ \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \not= \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}). \]

To prove further would require equality or inclusion that does not generally hold in arbitrary topological spaces.

So, the statement in its current form is false. The intersection of two alpha-open sets is not necessarily alpha-open.
