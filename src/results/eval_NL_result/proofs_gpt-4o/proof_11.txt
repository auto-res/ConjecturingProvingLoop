To prove the theorem, we need to show that the intersection of any two alpha-open sets in a topological space is alpha-open. 

Firstly, let's recall the definition of an alpha-open set. A subset \( A \) of a topological space \( X \) is said to be alpha-open if \( A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \), where \(\text{int}(A)\) denotes the interior of \( A \), and \(\overline{B}\) denotes the closure of \( B \).

Now, consider two alpha-open sets \( A \) and \( B \) in \( X \). By the definition of alpha-open sets, we have:

\[ A \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \]
\[ B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \]

We need to show that the intersection \( A \cap B \) is alpha-open, i.e.,

\[ A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \]

To explore this, let's use the properties of interior and closure operations. Notice the following general facts:

1. \(\text{int}(A \cap B) \subseteq \text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)\)

2. \(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)} \subseteq \overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}\)

3. \(\text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A) \cap \text{int}(B)}) \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \cap \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)})\)

From these facts, if \( x \in A \cap B \), then:

- Since \( x \in A \), by the alpha-open property of \( A \), \( x \in \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \).
- Similarly, since \( x \in B \), by the alpha-open property of \( B \), \( x \in \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \).

Thus, \( x \) is in both \(\text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)})\) and \(\text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)})\), meaning \( x \in \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \cap \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \).

Using the inclusion from above, we have:

\[ x \in \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A)}) \cap \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(B)}) \supseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \]

Therefore, every element \( x \) of \( A \cap B \) also belongs to \(\text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)})\). Hence:

\[ A \cap B \subseteq \text{int}(\overline{\text{int}(A \cap B)}) \]

which confirms that \( A \cap B \) is an alpha-open set.

Thus, the intersection of any two alpha-open sets is alpha-open. Therefore, the theorem is true.

Hence, we have proved the theorem.
